GNS-VLA: Neuro-Symbolic Vision-Language-Action Policies for Zero-Shot Long-Horizon Compositional Manipulation

Abstract

Zero-shot long-horizon manipulation remains difficult for Vision-Language-Action (VLA) Model policies because models that can execute individual primitives often fail to compose familiar primitives into unseen multi-step tasks. We study this problem as zero-shot long-horizon skill composition, where training includes individual primitives and limited combinations, while evaluation requires novel primitive sequences. We propose a neuro-symbolic VLA framework that combines scene-graph-based object-relation grounding with neural process monitoring, including neural state-machine-style modules for estimating stage progress and transition readiness. To evaluate this setting, we construct new long-horizon compositional benchmarks based on LIBERO and CALVIN spanning different numbers of subtasks, diverse subtask combinations, and multiple ordering permutations. Experiments show that the proposed framework achieves stronger compositional performance than competitive baselines, while detailed analyses reveal that failures mainly stem from language misunderstanding, limited out-of-distribution generalization to novel robot initializations, and sensitivity to additional visual perturbations. These results suggest that robust zero-shot long-horizon VLA policies requires not only stronger action policies, but also structured scene representations and observation-grounded process reasoning.

Main Contribution

We introduce GNS-VLA, a novel neuro-symbolic VLA framework which combines scene-graph-conditioned action learning, stage-language primitive supervision, and neural automaton-style process monitoring. The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:

  • A scene-graph-fused VLA policy, named GNS-VLA, is proposed that integrates object semantics, spatial relations, gripper-object interactions, and temporal scene dynamics into action generation.
  • A unified neuro-symbolic framework is introduced that combines task decomposition, stage-language primitive learning, GNS-VLA, and neural automaton process monitoring.
  • New zero-shot long-horizon compositional benchmarks are built based on LIBERO and CALVIN and show that the proposed framework improves autonomous skill stitching compared with generalist VLA and world-model baselines.

Key Methodology

  1. A hierarchical task and motion planning (TAMP) framework is proposed to utilize and generalize LfD methods for long-horizon manipulation tasks, with specific task and motion requirements.
  2. The spatial temporal logic (STL) and linear temporal logic is utilized to generate behavior tree (BT) in task planning and constrain representation in motion planning.
  3. An optimization method based on the quantification of STL fragments is proposed to generalize the DMPs trajectory with extensive STL-represented information, while preserving the natural dynamics learned from multiple demonstrations.
